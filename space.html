Planets of Our Solar System

From the scorching surface of Mercury to the icy plains of Pluto, explore the diverse worlds that orbit our Sun.

Our solar system is one of such many planetary systems. It consists of 8 planets in the order of Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune.

The Sun

Our star at the center of the solar system, providing light and heat to all planets.

Mercury

The smallest and innermost planet in the Solar System, with extreme temperature variations.

Venus

Similar in size to Earth but with a toxic atmosphere of carbon dioxide and sulfuric acid clouds.

Earth

Our home planet, the only known world to harbor life, with vast oceans and a protective atmosphere.

Mars

The Red Planet, with the largest volcano and canyon in the solar system, and possible ancient water.

Jupiter

The largest planet in our solar system, a gas giant with a Great Red Spot storm larger than Earth.

Saturn

Famous for its spectacular ring system, this gas giant has dozens of moons.

Uranus

An ice giant that rotates on its side, with a blue-green color from methane in its atmosphere.

Neptune

The windiest planet with the strongest winds in the solar system, and the coldest average temperature.

Galaxies Beyond Our Own

Explore the vast collections of stars, gas, and dust bound together by gravity that make up our universe.

Milky Way

Our home galaxy, a barred spiral galaxy containing 100-400 billion stars.

Andromeda

The nearest major galaxy to the Milky Way, on a collision course with our galaxy in about 4 billion years.

Triangulum

The third-largest member of our Local Group of galaxies, after Andromeda and the Milky Way.

Whirlpool

A classic spiral galaxy interacting with a smaller companion galaxy, located about 23 million light-years away.

Sombrero

A nearly edge-on galaxy with a bright nucleus, an unusually large central bulge, and a prominent dust lane.

Pinwheel

A face-on spiral galaxy about 21 million light-years away, with particularly well-defined spiral arms.

Our Solar System

The Solar System is the gravitationally bound system of the Sun and the objects that orbit it, either directly or indirectly. Of the objects that orbit the Sun directly, the largest are the eight planets, with the remainder being smaller objects, the dwarf planets and small Solar System bodies.

The Solar System formed 4.6 billion years ago from the gravitational collapse of a giant interstellar molecular cloud. The vast majority of the system's mass is in the Sun, with most of the remaining mass contained in Jupiter.

Age

4.6 billion years

Planets

8 (Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune)

Dwarf Planets

5 (Pluto, Ceres, Haumea, Makemake, Eris)

Distance to Nearest Star

4.24 light years (Proxima Centauri)

The Milky Way Galaxy

The Milky Way is the galaxy that contains our Solar System. The name describes the galaxy's appearance from Earth: a hazy band of light seen in the night sky formed from stars that cannot be individually distinguished by the naked eye.

The Milky Way is a barred spiral galaxy with an estimated diameter between 150,000 and 200,000 light-years. It is estimated to contain 100-400 billion stars and at least that number of planets.

Type

Barred spiral galaxy

Diameter

150,000–200,000 light-years

Stars

100–400 billion

Age

13.51 billion years

Andromeda Galaxy

The Andromeda Galaxy, also known as Messier 31 or M31, is a barred spiral galaxy approximately 2.5 million light-years from Earth and the nearest major galaxy to the Milky Way. Its name stems from the area of the sky in which it appears, the constellation of Andromeda.

The Andromeda Galaxy is approaching the Milky Way at about 110 kilometres per second (68 mi/s) and in about 4 billion years it will collide with the Milky Way.

Barred spiral galaxy

Diameter

220,000 light-years

Stars

1 trillion

Distance

The Sun

The Sun is the star at the center of the Solar System. It is a nearly perfect sphere of hot plasma, heated to incandescence by nuclear fusion reactions in its core, radiating the energy mainly as visible light and infrared radiation.

The Sun is by far the most important source of energy for life on Earth. Its diameter is about 1.39 million kilometers (864,000 miles), or 109 times that of Earth, and its mass is about 330,000 times that of Earth.

Type

Yellow dwarf (G2V)

Age

4.6 billion years

Diameter

1.39 million km

Surface Temp

Mercury

Mercury is the smallest and innermost planet in the Solar System. Its orbit around the Sun takes 87.97 Earth days, the shortest of all the planets in the Solar System. It is named after the Roman god Mercurius (Mercury), god of commerce, messenger of the gods, and mediator between gods and mortals.

Mercury has no natural satellites. It has a very thin atmosphere consisting of atoms blasted off its surface by the solar wind. Because Mercury is so close to the Sun, it has extreme temperature variations: 430°C (800°F) during the day and -180°C (-290°F) at night.

Terrestrial planet

Diameter
